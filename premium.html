Traditional Couscous

The national dish of Tunisia

Ingredients (Serves 6):

‚Ä¢ 500g fine couscous
‚Ä¢ 500g lamb or chicken, cut into pieces
‚Ä¢ 2 large onions, sliced
‚Ä¢ 3 carrots, cut into chunks
‚Ä¢ 2 potatoes, quartered
‚Ä¢ 200g chickpeas, soaked overnight
‚Ä¢ 2 tbsp harissa paste
‚Ä¢ 1 tsp ground cumin
‚Ä¢ 1 tsp ground coriander

Preparation Steps:

1 Prepare the couscous

Place couscous in a large bowl, sprinkle with salted water, and let steam for 30 minutes in a couscoussier.

2 Cook the meat and vegetables

In a large pot, brown the meat with onions, add spices, then add vegetables and chickpeas with water. Simmer for 45 minutes.

3 Add harissa and final cooking

Stir in harissa paste and cook for another 15 minutes until vegetables are tender.

4 Serve traditionally

Fluff the couscous with butter, serve in a large platter with meat and vegetables arranged on top.

Chef's Tip: The key to perfect couscous is steaming it three times, fluffing with your hands between each steaming.

Tunisian Brik

Crispy pastry with runny egg center

Ingredients (Makes 6):

‚Ä¢ 6 sheets of brik pastry (or filo dough)
‚Ä¢ 6 fresh eggs
‚Ä¢ 200g canned tuna, drained
‚Ä¢ 2 potatoes, boiled and mashed
‚Ä¢ 1 onion, finely chopped
‚Ä¢ 2 tbsp parsley, chopped
‚Ä¢ 1 tsp harissa paste
‚Ä¢ Oil for frying

Preparation Steps:

1 Prepare the filling

Mix tuna, mashed potatoes, onion, parsley, and harissa. Season with salt and pepper.

2 Assemble the brik

Place a spoonful of filling on each pastry sheet, create a well, and crack an egg into it.

3 Fold and seal

Fold the pastry into a triangle, sealing the edges carefully to keep the egg inside.

4 Fry to perfection

Deep fry in hot oil (180¬∞C) for 2-3 minutes until golden and crispy. Serve immediately.

Chef's Tip: The secret is keeping the yolk runny! Don't overcook - the egg should flow when you cut into the brik.

Cultural Etiquette & Traditions

Understand Tunisian customs, social norms, and cultural practices to show respect and build meaningful connections.

ü§ù Social Etiquette

Greetings

Use "Aslema" for hello and "Yaishek" for thank you. Handshakes are common, but wait for the other person to initiate.

Hospitality

Tunisians are incredibly hospitable. Always accept tea or coffee when offered - it's a sign of respect.

Dining Customs

Eat with your right hand. Wait for the host to start eating. Compliment the food generously.

Photography

Always ask permission before photographing people, especially in rural areas and markets.

üéâ Festivals & Celebrations

Ramadan

Respect fasting hours, avoid eating/drinking in public during daylight. Evening iftar meals are social occasions.

Eid Celebrations

Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha are major holidays. Families gather, share meals, and exchange gifts.

Carthage Festival

Annual summer festival featuring music, theater, and cultural performances in the ancient amphitheater.

Sahara Festival

Celebrated in Douz, showcasing Bedouin culture with camel races, music, and traditional crafts.

üïå Religious & Cultural Sites

Mosque Etiquette

Remove shoes, dress modestly (cover shoulders and knees), and speak quietly. Non-Muslims may not enter all areas.

Historical Sites

Show respect at ancient ruins and archaeological sites. These are sacred cultural heritage locations.

Medina Exploration

Get lost in the medina's maze-like streets, but be respectful of residential areas and local shops.

Traditional Crafts

Watch artisans at work, ask questions respectfully, and consider purchasing to support local craftspeople.

üí° Insider Tips

Bargaining

Expected in markets and souks. Start at 50% of the asking price and negotiate with a smile.

Tipping

10% is standard in restaurants. Round up taxi fares and tip guides and porters.

Dress Code

Beachwear is fine at resorts, but dress modestly in towns and religious areas.

Language

Arabic is official, French widely spoken. Learning basic Tunisian Arabic phrases is greatly appreciated.
